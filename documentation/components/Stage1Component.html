  <h2>Introduction</h2>
  <p>To design a sampling process, you should first have a clear idea of your main objective. This is, your overall aim for
    the activity that you are doing sampling for.</p>

  <p>
    Developing sampling designs is about making decisions that will define how you will collect data and how you will estimate
    the value of one or a set of characteristics of a population. We sometimes call those characteristics:
    <glossary-link slug="indicator">indicators</glossary-link>. This guide has been designed to help with this type of objective. This may happen for example
    when profiling populations, setting
    <glossary-link slug="baseline">baselines</glossary-link>
    or assessing the value of indicators at
    <glossary-link slug="endline">end-line</glossary-link> or midline points. Common examples can be seen in the
    <a href="/#//stage-1/resources">resources</a> tab.
  </p>

  <p>In some cases a study may be established to compare interventions and require a
    <glossary-link slug="quasi-experiments">quasi-experimental</glossary-link> or
    <glossary-link slug="experiments">experimental</glossary-link> approach. This tool has not been aimed to support decisions for this type of design, but
    it will provide pointers to resources and links that may help with the question of how many times a treatment should
    be repeated.</p>

  <!-- q1.1 -->
  <p>
    The first question you will answer broadly separates these two types of objectives. The first answer provided is for people
    conducting a survey to estimate a characteristic of a population. The second answer is for those wishing to evaluate
    the comparative benefits of a set of options while controlling external factors, that is, setting an experimental or
    a quasi-experimental study. If you are not sure which is the correct answer for you, have a look at the examples provided.
  </p>
