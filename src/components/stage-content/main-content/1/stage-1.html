Introduction

To effectively design a sampling process, you should first have a clear idea of your main objective. This is your overall aim for the activity that you are doing sampling for.

Developing sampling designs is about making decisions that will define how you will collect data and how you will estimate the value of one or a set of characteristics of a population. We sometimes call those characteristics indicators. This guide has been designed to help with this type of objective. Common examples can be seen in the resources tab. This may happen for example when profiling populations, setting baselines or assessing the value of indicators at end-line or midline points.

In some cases a study may be established to compare interventions and require a quasi-experimental or experimental approach. This tool has not been aimed to support decisions for this type of design but it will provide pointers to useful resources and links that may help with the question of how many times a treatment should be repeated.

The first question you will answer broadly separates these two types of objectives. The first answer is for people conducting a survey to estimate a characteristics of a population. The second is for those wishing to evaluate the impact of an intervention while controlling external factors. If you are not sure which is the correct answer for you, have a look at the examples provided.
